ABOUT

  • Background

    • Over the past 30 years, the representation of women directors in the film industry has been shaped by societal movements, industry changes, and global trends. Despite these developments, the industry remains male-dominated, and women continue to be significantly underrepresented in the filmmaking workforce.

  • Problem

    • Women directors remain significantly underrepresented in the film industry, particularly in mainstream, high-budget productions. Also Independent films are often overlooked in favor of mainstream productions, yet they play a critical role in enabling diverse voices, including women and marginalized groups.

  • Our Goal

    • To discover trends in the role of female directors in cinema by analyzing their activity across different countries and examining movie databases for specific periods of study. This will include exploring the role of independent cinema as a pathway for women directors. The research will uncover patterns of representation, contributions, and shifts over time.

Data Preparation

Data Preparation

We examined the data models of Wikidata and DBpedia regarding female directors, films, genres, and release dates.

Data Collection: Release dates, the number of films directed by women, countries, and the number of women directors were collected from Wikidata and DBpedia. We queried both Wikidata and DBpedia SPARQL endpoints, reconciled the data, and removed duplicates. Despite this process, the final dataset was significantly reduced, resulting in only 109 matches by director labels (e.g., Lana Wachowski). This reduction contrasts with the fact that Wikidata alone lists 10,342 female directors! DBpedia Issues: DBpedia presents challenges with gender identity. While it uses the RDF label "female director", many entries for the gender property remain unknown or unpopulated. As a result, DBpedia cannot be relied upon for fully reconciling data with Wikidata due to its significant lack of information. IMDb Limitations: IMDb does not provide comprehensive film data. It offers subsets of its database for personal and non-commercial use, but these subsets are limited. The broadest available datasets only include: titleType (string), startYear (YYYY), runtimeMinutes, genres (string array). This limitation prevents access to complete information about films directed by women, further constraining the analysis.

The Europeana Data Model (EDM) does not include a specific property to denote the gender of creators, such as schema:gender. The EDM primarily focuses on properties like dc:creator to identify the creator of a cultural heritage object, without detailing attributes like gender. This is evident in the EDM Mapping Guidelines, which outline the properties and classes used within the model. The guidelines detail various metadata elements but do not specify a property for capturing the gender of creators. Additionally, the EDM documentation available on Europeana’s professional portal provides comprehensive information about the model’s structure and elements. A review of these resources confirms the absence of a gender-specific property for creators. Therefore, while EDM facilitates the integration and sharing of cultural heritage metadata across Europe, it currently lacks provisions for recording the gender of creators within its standard schema.
